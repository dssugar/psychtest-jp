# Phase 1 Report: Rosenberg Self-Esteem Scale (RSES)

## Basic Information

- **Full Name**: Rosenberg Self-Esteem Scale
- **Abbreviation**: RSES
- **Developer**: Morris Rosenberg
- **Year**: 1965
- **Publication**: Rosenberg, M. (1965). *Society and the adolescent self-image*. Princeton, NJ: Princeton University Press.
- **DOI/ISBN**: ISBN 978-0-691-64913-1

## Psychometric Properties

### Items and Format
- **Items**: 10 items
- **Response Format**: 4-point Likert scale (1-4)
  - 1: Strongly Disagree
  - 2: Disagree
  - 3: Agree
  - 4: Strongly Agree
- **Reverse Items**: 5 items (Items 3, 5, 8, 9, 10)
- **Score Range**: 10-40 points
- **Administration Time**: ~3 minutes

### Reliability

#### Internal Consistency (Cronbach's α)
- **Original study (1965)**: α = 0.77 (minimum Coefficient of Reproducibility ≥ 0.90)
- **Meta-analytic evidence (Gray-Little et al., 1997)**: α ≈ 0.80
- **Range across studies**: α = 0.72-0.88
- **Recent studies**:
  - U.S. community samples (Sinclair et al., 2010): α = 0.85
  - International samples (Wongpakaran & Wongpakaran, 2012): α = 0.88
  - Thai university students (n=664): α = 0.86

**Summary**: Excellent internal consistency across diverse populations.

#### Test-Retest Reliability
- **2-week interval** (Silber & Tippett, 1965): r = 0.85
- **7-month interval** (Shorkey & Whiteman, 1978): r = 0.63

**Interpretation**: High short-term stability; moderate long-term stability (expected for a state-trait construct).

### Validity

#### Construct Validity
- **Factor Structure**: Uni-dimensional model with method effects (positively vs. negatively worded items)
- **Confirmatory Factor Analysis** (revised version, n=187):
  - χ² = 29.19 (df=19, p=0.063) ✓ Good fit
  - CFI = 0.987 ✓ Excellent fit
  - TLI = 0.969 ✓ Excellent fit
  - RMSEA = 0.054 ✓ Good fit
  - SRMR = 0.040 ✓ Excellent fit
- **Factor loadings**: 0.277-0.814 (acceptable to strong)

#### Concurrent Validity
- **Attachment anxiety**: r = -0.23 (p<0.01) ✓ Expected negative correlation
- **Depression**: r = -0.30 (p<0.01) ✓ Expected negative correlation

#### International Validation
- **Schmitt & Allik (2005)**: Large international study across 53 nations
  - Factor structure generally invariant across cultures
  - Reliability substantial overall
  - Effective across diverse geographic and cultural contexts

### Citations and Impact

- **Google Scholar citations (2020)**: 40,000+ citations
- **Research impact**: ~50% of empirical studies on self-esteem in major journals use RSES
- **Status**: Most widely used self-esteem instrument globally

## Psychological Layer

**Outcome (成果)** - Self-Esteem

### Rationale:
Self-esteem is classified as an **outcome** (rather than trait, state, or skill) because:

1. **Product of other factors**: Self-esteem results from the interaction of personality traits (e.g., neuroticism, extraversion), life experiences, and current psychological states
2. **Evaluative construct**: It represents an evaluative judgment about the self, which is influenced by multiple psychological processes
3. **Influenced by interventions**: Unlike stable traits, self-esteem can be improved through therapy, skill-building, and positive experiences
4. **Relationship with well-being**: Self-esteem is better conceptualized as an outcome that influences (and is influenced by) mental health, rather than as a fundamental trait

### Research Support:
- **Trait-State-Outcome model** (Steyer et al., 1999): Self-esteem shows both trait-like stability and state-like variability
- **Self-esteem as buffer**: Functions as an outcome that protects against stress and promotes well-being (Rosenberg, 1965)
- **Developmental changes**: Self-esteem changes across the lifespan in response to life events and developmental tasks (Robins & Trzesniewski, 2005)
